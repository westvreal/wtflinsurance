Full Water Coverage vs. Limited Water: What Your Policy Actually Covers

By WTFLinsurance Team April 3, 2026 8 min read

Here's a scenario that plays out in Florida every single week: a homeowner discovers water pooling in their kitchen. Turns out, the dishwasher supply line failed and water has been seeping under the cabinets. They file a claim, fully expecting their homeowners insurance to cover the damage.

The adjuster shows up, takes one look, and delivers the bad news: "This is gradual water damage. Your policy doesn't cover it."

$12,000 in flooring and cabinet damage. Zero dollars from insurance.

The problem wasn't that they didn't have insurance. The problem was they had limited water coverage and didn't even know it.

What You'll Learn

This guide breaks down what "water damage" actually means on your policy, the gap between limited and full water coverage, and the endorsements you need to close the gaps — before something leaks, backs up, or bursts.

Limited vs. Full Water Coverage: The Big Picture

Most homeowners assume "water damage" is just one thing. It's not. Your policy treats different types of water damage very differently. Here's the breakdown:

Limited Water Coverage
Full Water Coverage
What's included

Only sudden and accidental water damage: a pipe that bursts, an appliance that overflows unexpectedly.

What's included

Sudden/accidental plus water backup, sewer/drain backup, service line damage, and enhanced mold coverage.

What's excluded

Sewer backup, gradual leaks, water seepage, service line breaks, floods, and most mold damage.

What's excluded

External flooding (requires separate flood policy). Everything else is typically covered.

The risk

You're protected from the obvious stuff but exposed to the most common and expensive types of water damage.

The cost

Endorsements typically add $50–$150/year total. A fraction of the claims they cover.

The Endorsements That Close the Gap

The good news: you don't need a different policy. You just need to add the right endorsements (add-ons) to your existing homeowners policy. Here are the ones that matter:

Standard

Sudden & Accidental Water

Pipe bursts, appliance overflows, accidental discharge. Already included in your base policy — no action needed.

Add Endorsement

Water Backup / Sewer Backup

Covers damage when sewers, drains, or sump pumps back up into your home. Typically $5K–$25K in coverage. Costs $40–$75/year.

Add Endorsement

Service Line Coverage

Covers the underground pipes running from the street to your house. Aging pipes in older Florida homes are a major risk. Costs $25–$50/year.

Add Endorsement

Enhanced Mold Coverage

Standard policies cap mold at $10K or less. This endorsement bumps it to $25K–$50K. Costs $20–$40/year. Critical in Florida's humidity.

Separate Policy

Flood Insurance

External flooding (storm surge, rising water, heavy rain accumulation) is NEVER covered by homeowners insurance. Requires a separate NFIP or private flood policy.

Not Available

Gradual / Maintenance Damage

Slow leaks, seepage, and damage from deferred maintenance are excluded by every policy. Your only protection is regular home maintenance and inspections.

Same Storm, Different Outcomes

Meet Carlos and Diana. They live on the same street in Jacksonville. Same neighborhood, same style house, similar policies. After a heavy rainstorm, both of their homes take on water through backed-up sewer lines. Here's how their claims play out:

Carlos: Limited Water Coverage

Carlos has a standard homeowners policy with no water backup endorsement. The sewer backup floods his first floor with 3 inches of contaminated water.

Flooring replacement: $6,200
Drywall & baseboards: $3,400
Professional cleanup (Category 3 water): $2,800
Damaged furniture & belongings: $2,100
Total damage: $14,500
Insurance payout: $0 (sewer backup excluded)
Carlos pays: $14,500 out of pocket

His policy covers burst pipes and appliance overflows, but sewer backup is specifically excluded. Carlos is responsible for the entire bill.

Diana: Full Water Coverage

Diana has the same base policy but added a water backup endorsement ($25K limit) and enhanced mold coverage. She pays an extra $95/year for both.

− $1,000 deductible
Insurance payout: $13,500
Diana pays: $1,000 (just her deductible)

Diana's water backup endorsement kicks in and covers the full claim minus her deductible. Her extra $95/year in premiums just saved her $13,500.

Why This Matters Even More in Florida

Florida's climate and infrastructure make water damage endorsements more important here than almost anywhere else in the country:

  • Aging infrastructure: Many Florida homes sit on clay or limestone soil with sewer lines 30–50 years old. Tree roots infiltrate pipes, and joints crack. Service line breaks are extremely common.
  • Hurricane season: Heavy rainfall overwhelms municipal sewer systems, causing backups even in homes that don't flood. Your homeowners policy covers wind damage but NOT the sewer water that backs up through your drains.
  • Humidity and mold: Any water intrusion in Florida's climate creates a mold risk within 24–48 hours. Standard mold limits of $10,000 can be eaten up by a single bathroom remediation. Enhanced mold coverage is critical.
  • Slab foundations: Most Florida homes are built on concrete slabs with plumbing running underneath. A service line break can mean jackhammering your floors — easily a $10,000+ repair.
  • Older homes: Cast iron and galvanized steel pipes in pre-1980s homes are reaching end-of-life. When they fail, it's rarely sudden — it's gradual, and gradual isn't covered.

What to Ask Your Agent Today

Your Water Coverage Checklist

1
Do I have water backup / sewer backup coverage?

If not, add it. Ask for at least $25,000 in coverage. It typically costs $40–$75/year.

2
Do I have service line coverage?

Covers underground pipes from the street to your house. Especially important for homes built before 1990. Costs $25–$50/year.

3
What's my mold coverage limit?

If it's $10,000 or less, ask about enhanced mold coverage. In Florida, $25K–$50K is recommended. Costs $20–$40/year.

4
Does my policy cover "sudden and accidental" discharge only?

That's standard. It means gradual leaks are excluded. This isn't something you can endorse around — it's a maintenance issue. Inspect your plumbing regularly.

5
Do I have flood insurance?

Homeowners insurance never covers flooding. If you're anywhere near water in Florida, you need a separate flood policy. Read our flood insurance guide.

The Bottom Line

For roughly $85–$150/year in extra endorsements, you can turn a policy full of water damage gaps into comprehensive coverage that actually protects you. Here's what to remember:

Limited Water Coverage

Only covers sudden/accidental water damage. Sewer backup, service lines, and mold above $10K are all on you. The most expensive claims aren't covered.

Full Water Coverage

Add 3 endorsements (water backup + service line + mold) for about $85–$150/year total. Closes nearly every gap except flooding and maintenance.

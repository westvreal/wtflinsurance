⛵ Boat Insurance Knowledge Base

Protect your vessel on Florida's waterways with the right marine insurance coverage.

What Does Boat Insurance Cover?

Boat insurance (also called watercraft or marine insurance) protects you financially if your boat is damaged, stolen, or involved in an accident. Florida doesn't require boat insurance by law, but most marinas, lenders, and common sense do.

A standard boat policy typically includes:

Hull Coverage (Physical Damage)

Covers damage to the boat itself — the hull, motor, sails, and permanently attached equipment. This applies whether the boat is on the water, in storage, or being towed. Policies are written as either agreed value (you and the insurer agree on the boat's worth upfront) or actual cash value (depreciated value at the time of loss).

Liability Coverage

Protects you if you injure someone or damage their property while operating your boat. In Florida, where waterways are crowded year-round, this is critical. We recommend at least $300,000 in liability coverage.

Medical Payments

Covers medical expenses for you and your passengers if someone is injured in a boating accident, regardless of fault.

Uninsured Watercraft Coverage

Covers injuries caused by another boater who doesn't have insurance. Think of it like uninsured motorist coverage for the water.

Fuel Spill Liability

Covers the cost of cleaning up a fuel spill from your boat. Environmental cleanup is extremely expensive, and you're legally responsible for any spill from your vessel.

Towing and Assistance

Covers the cost of on-water towing if your boat breaks down. A single tow on open water can cost $500–$2,000+, making this coverage well worth the small premium.

Florida-Specific

Florida has more registered boats than any other state — over 900,000. While boat insurance isn't legally required, lenders will require it if you finance your boat, and most marinas require proof of liability coverage for slip rentals.

Types of Boat Policies

The right policy depends on what you're insuring:

  • Small boat / personal watercraft (PWC): Jet skis, kayaks with motors, small fishing boats. Often the most affordable policies ($200–$500/year).
  • Powerboat: Center consoles, bass boats, pontoons, bowriders. Mid-range pricing ($500–$2,000/year depending on size and value).
  • Sailboat: Unique coverage needs for rigging, sails, and longer voyages. Rates vary widely.
  • Yacht: Vessels over 27 feet typically need specialized yacht policies with higher limits and crew coverage.

How Much Does Boat Insurance Cost in Florida?

As a general rule, expect to pay about 1–3% of your boat's value per year. Here's what that looks like:

  • $10,000 fishing boat: $150–$350/year
  • $30,000 center console: $400–$900/year
  • $75,000 cabin cruiser: $800–$2,000/year
  • $150,000+ yacht: $2,000–$5,000+/year

Factors that affect your rate include the boat's value, age, type, horsepower, where you keep it, your boating experience, and your navigation area (coastal vs. inland).

Agreed Value vs. Actual Cash Value

This is the single most important decision on your boat policy:

  • Agreed value: You and the insurer agree on the boat's value when you buy the policy. If it's a total loss, you get that full amount. This is the better option for most boat owners.
  • Actual cash value (ACV): The insurer pays the depreciated market value at the time of loss. This means your payout decreases every year as the boat ages. ACV policies are cheaper but can leave you significantly short in a claim.

What's NOT Covered?

Standard boat insurance typically excludes:

  • Normal wear and tear — maintenance is your responsibility
  • Mechanical or electrical breakdown — unless caused by a covered event
  • Marine life damage — barnacles, osmotic blistering, etc.
  • Racing — requires a separate endorsement
  • Commercial use — if you charter your boat for income, you need commercial marine insurance
  • Named storms — some Florida policies exclude hurricane damage or have separate hurricane deductibles

How to Save on Boat Insurance

  • Complete a boating safety course (5–15% discount)
  • Install safety equipment: fire extinguishers, GPS, VHF radio
  • Choose a higher deductible
  • Bundle with your auto or home insurance
  • Maintain a clean boating record
  • Store your boat on land during hurricane season
  • Ask about lay-up periods (reduced rates when the boat isn't in use)
Hurricane Season Tip

Florida boat owners should have a documented hurricane plan. Many insurers offer discounts for haul-out agreements with marinas. If your boat is damaged because you didn't follow your hurricane plan, your claim could be denied.
